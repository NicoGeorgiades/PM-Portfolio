Professional Narrative

Bridging Dynamic Operations & Capital Efficiency

My background as an operations and project management practitioner is built on real-world execution. During my undergraduate studies, I co-founded and systematically scaled a bootstrapped furniture retail and logistics business. Over its lifecycle, the venture expanded fluidly from a simple residential garage proof-of-concept into a multi-warehouse enterprise managing bulk international container freight.

Operating under a highly lean footprint of just a three-person total headcount, I directed multi-site facility tenant improvements, optimized logistics fleet overhead, and synchronized slow-moving maritime supply chains with daily retail cash velocity. In the venture's final phase, I single-handedly managed the strategic wind-down, executing lease terminations, asset liquidations, and liability mitigation.

This intensive experience developed my capability to manage severe dependency and capital risks under tight resource constraints. Today, I combine this operational foundation with modern credentials (CAPM® and Google AI) to design, evaluate, and demonstrate advanced system architectures and workflow optimizations retrospectively.

Milestone / Phase Est. Duration Critical Dependencies PM Risk Mitigation Note
Phase 1: Sourcing & Capital Allocation
M1.1: Production Sourcing & PO Initiation 1–2 Weeks Aggregated capital from local used furniture sourcing velocity; signed procurement agreement with overseas manufacturer. Capital Lock-Up Mitigation: Upfront deposit timed precisely with cyclical high-volume used inventory liquidations to prevent draining daily operational working capital.
M1.2: Container Deposit Clearance & Factory Allocation 3–5 Days Clearance of 30%–50% wire transfer deposit; factory confirmation of production slot scheduling. Just-In-Time (JIT) Funding: Used furniture sourcing runs are accelerated in the weeks leading up to this milestone to build a cash reserve buffer specifically for the wire transfer.
Phase 2: Manufacturing & Quality Control
M2.1: Production Lifecycle Execution 4–6 Weeks Consistent raw material availability at origin factory; uninterrupted cash flow from local used retail operations to cover ongoing fixed warehouse lease overhead. Portfolio Balancing: Because manufacturing lead times completely freeze this capital, the core used-furniture reverse-logistics operation is run at maximum throughput to pay the bills and keep the physical storefront active.
M2.2: QC Approval & Freight Booking Acceptance 3–7 Days Factory passing quality control inspection; shipping line container allocation acceptance (Origin Port). Freight Cost Controls: For split-consignment testing, this gate requires rigorous partner alignment to synchronize final shipping documents and divide origin logistics fees cleanly.
Phase 3: Transit & Maritime Logistics
M3.1: Origin Port Handling & Vessel Departure 5–10 Days Ocean Bill of Lading (BOL) generation; wire transfer clearance for the remaining balance of the goods prior to cargo release. Liquidity Management: This milestone represents peak capital strain. Sourcing teams prioritize hyper-local, fast-turning used items to rapidly generate the cash needed to clear the final invoice before the ship sails.
M3.2: Ocean Freight Transit & Track-and-Trace 20–35 Days Stable maritime conditions; accurate ETA updates from freight forwarders. Buffer Management: Capital is entirely dead while at sea. The PM leverages this fixed window to aggressively clear out old used stock in the warehouse, physically carving out floor space for the incoming new inventory.
Phase 4: Customs, Inland Freight, & Arrival
M4.1: Port Arrival, Customs Entry, & ISF Clearance 3–7 Days Submission of commercial invoice, packing lists, and customs documentation; payment of customs duties and port harbor fees. Demurrage Risk Avoidance: Port storage and demurrage fees accumulate rapidly. Cash flow targets from the storefront must prioritize liquidity to guarantee immediate duty payments upon port arrival.
M4.2: Rail Intermodal & Inland Truck Drayage 5–12 Days Availability of inland chassis and regional rail/freight capacity; coordination with inbound semi-truck carrier. Carrier Management: Establish a strict communication protocol with the freight brokerage to receive a minimum 48-hour delivery window, ensuring the lean warehouse team can be dynamically scheduled.
Phase 5: Multi-Warehouse Receiving & Staging
M5.1: Semi-Truck Gate Arrival & Hand-Unloading 1–2 Hours Full deployment of internal staff for manual de-vanning. Detention Fee Mitigation: Because shipments are unloaded completely by hand without automated equipment, team shifts are optimized for rapid, continuous labor to beat the carrier's 1-hour free time window.
M5.2: De-Crating, Sorting, & Quality Audit 1–2 Days Successful, damage-free unboxing; matching physical item counts against the factory packing list. Claim Management: Immediate documentation of any transit damages is required to fulfill warranty protocols, safeguarding the venture against margin loss on new items.
M5.3: Floor Staging & Showroom Optimization 2–3 Days Completed preparation of the showroom (e.g., renovated flooring, layout adjustments); strategic balancing of showroom floor space. Floor Space Velocity: Inventory is segregated into the hybrid matrix: high-turnover used furniture is staged to continue paying fixed operational costs, while the high-ticket new manufacturer lines are aggressively showcased to drive the scalable future of the enterprise.
